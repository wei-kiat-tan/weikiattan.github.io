Decades After the War Ended, the Ground Still Remembers

The Cambodian Civil War left behind more than painful memories. Millions of landmines and explosive remnants of war (ERW) continue to threaten communities today — a legacy of fear buried just beneath the surface.

Historical Context

A Nation Shaped by Conflict

The Cambodian Civil War (1967-1975) and its aftermath left deep scars on the nation. What followed was decades of insurgency, foreign intervention, and a landscape permanently altered by millions of unexploded ordnances.

1967-1975
Cambodian Civil War
Conflict between the Khmer Rouge, the Kingdom of Cambodia, and later the Khmer Republic devastated the nation and paved the way for one of history's darkest chapters.
1969-1973
Operation Menu & Freedom Deal
The United States conducted extensive bombing campaigns across Eastern Cambodia, dropping an estimated 2.7 million tons of ordnance — more than was dropped on Japan during all of World War II.
1975-1979
Khmer Rouge Regime
The brutal genocide claimed nearly 2 million lives and created a massive refugee crisis throughout Southeast Asia.
1979-1989
Vietnamese Occupation & K5 Plan
Vietnamese forces overthrew the Khmer Rouge, but a new threat emerged: the K5 defense line — a 700km barrier of landmines along the Thai border to prevent insurgent attacks.
1998-Present
Living with the Legacy
Though active conflict has ended, Cambodians continue to discover and fall victim to unexploded ordnances scattered across their homeland.

The Geography of Danger

Why Are Mines Concentrated Along the Thai Border?

The map reveals a striking pattern: mine incidents cluster heavily in provinces facing Thailand, not Vietnam. This is the direct legacy of the K5 defense plan.

During the Vietnamese occupation, Cambodian civilians were forced to construct a "bamboo wall" of landmines stretching 700km along the Thai border — designed to prevent Khmer Rouge forces from launching attacks from their Thai sanctuaries.

The Scattered Legacy

ERW: The Invisible Threat Across the Nation

Unlike landmines, Explosive Remnants of War (ERW) are scattered across the entire country. This pattern reflects the extensive U.S. bombing campaigns of the 1970s.

Operation Freedom Deal targeted eastern provinces, while Operation Menu focused on border areas. Decades later, unexploded bombs continue to surface in farmers' fields and children's playgrounds.

Critical Finding

The eastern province of Kampong Cham shows particularly high ERW incidents — a direct result of the relentless bombing campaigns that targeted this region.

Rural vs Urban

The Countryside Bears the Burden

The impact of landmines and ERW is not evenly distributed. Rural communities, where agriculture drives the economy, face far greater exposure to danger than urban areas.

"Rural areas are more exposed to dangers from landmines and ERW as the majority of Cambodia's economic and daily activities are in agriculture."

Rural Areas

Farmers clearing land, children herding livestock, families collecting firewood — daily activities in the countryside carry inherent risk. Most incidents occur in agricultural settings where ordnances lie hidden just beneath the soil.

Living with Fear

The Psychological Burden

Beyond physical danger, landmines and ERW create a pervasive atmosphere of fear. Survey data from 2009-2014 reveals how proximity to ordnances shapes the daily anxiety of Cambodian communities.

Fear by Province

Not All Provinces Fear Equally

Provinces along the Thai border — Battambang, Banteay Meanchey, and Pailin — report the highest fear levels. These areas were most heavily mined during the K5 plan.

Meanwhile, central and southern provinces show lower anxiety levels, correlating with reduced mine presence in these regions.

Proximity Matters

The Closer the Threat, the Greater the Fear?

Surprisingly, the relationship between proximity and fear is not absolute. While communities living "very near" to known ordnances report higher fear, many provinces maintain medium fear levels even when proximity is far.

Interesting Finding

Preah Vihear registers higher counts of medium fear level even when proximity is far — reaching close to 300 respondents. This suggests fear is shaped by historical memory, not just current proximity.

The Battambang Case

High Fear in the K5 Zone

Battambang consistently shows high fear levels for very near, near, and even far proximity to ordnances. Its location near the K5 defense line produces understandable paranoia among communities there.

Nearby Pailin, however, shows lower fear levels despite similar geography — likely due to inconsistent survey coverage rather than actual reduced danger.

Eastern Cambodia

The Bombing Campaign's Echo

Kampong Cham shows notably high fear levels when proximity is very near or near. This eastern province bore the brunt of U.S. bombing campaigns, and communities remain worried about encountering unexploded ordnance.

High fear counts in this region are higher than medium fear — a pattern distinct from western provinces, suggesting different sources of trauma.

Conclusion

A Legacy That Demands Action

The Cambodian Civil War's legacy of landmines and ERW continues to impact communities to a large extent. This analysis reveals three key findings that underscore the ongoing humanitarian challenge.

Geographic Concentration

Mines cluster along the Thai border (K5 legacy), while ERW scatter nationwide from U.S. bombing campaigns.

Rural Vulnerability

Agricultural communities face the greatest danger, with daily activities putting farmers and families at constant risk.

Psychological Impact

Fear levels vary by province and are shaped by historical memory, not just current proximity to ordnances.

"Until international and local efforts continue or step up their work to clear the bleak legacy of the Cambodian Civil War, more Cambodians will continue to fall victim to landmines and ERW."

Path Forward

Education and supervision are key — particularly for children, who are the most vulnerable group. Preventing complacency among communities as they live with landmines and ERW requires sustained awareness and international support.
