Does Working Harder
Make You Happier?

In South Korea, they call it "gwarosa". In Japan, "karoshi" — death by overwork. But what if 7 years of data across 45 countries tells a different story?

Introduction

The Work-Happiness Paradox

Long working hours are negatively associated with stress, depression, and low subjective well-being. However, not every economy shares the same narrative. Some economies achieve better happiness results despite working longer hours.

Could better working conditions justify working longer? Could an economy with higher GDP per capita provide a more advantageous starting point for happiness? Is happiness dependent on the region economies are located due to differences in working culture? We investigate this complex relationship between work and happiness using data from OECD, World Bank, and the World Happiness Report.

The relationship between work and happiness is not significant to a large extent. Better working conditions do not necessarily justify workers working longer.

Finding 01

Does Economic Environment Affect Work & Happiness?

There are 2 questions being asked. First, are people happier if they were in a country or economy with much higher Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita than others? If so, would they be willing to work for longer hours because they are in a better economic environment?

A country with higher GDP per capita implies better standard of living, better access to modern infrastructure and quality of life. With these conditions, an individual in a country with higher GDP per capita would be happier. However, there is no free lunch — in exchange to enjoy these benefits, would they work longer?

Observations: 5 Data Clusters

Figure 9: Log GDP per Capita against Annual Average Working Hours

From Figure 9, there are 5 data clusters:

1. Netherlands: Less than 30 annual average working hours — lowest in dataset.
2. Main Cluster: Denmark, Ireland, Luxembourg, Chile and majority — above 30 to less than 45 hours.
3. Colombia: Distinct cluster with longer hours.
4. Turkey: Long hours with lower GDP per capita.
5. Singapore: High GDP per capita but with working hours above 40.

Netherlands is notable for having the lowest annual average working hours but still achieve higher log GDP per capita than others. On the other side, Singapore achieves higher log GDP per capita at a cost of much longer working hours above 40. At the extreme end, Turkey and Colombia worked a range of more than 45 hours but less than 50 and achieve lower log GDP per capita than Netherlands and Singapore.

Insights

Key Finding

People living in countries with higher GDP per capita tend to be happier and have a more positive outlook in life. Not only do they have better life ladder thinking they can achieve best possible outcome in life, but they also achieve higher positive emotions rating and lower negative emotions rating.

Interestingly, some countries like Luxembourg and Ireland can achieve this without needing to work for long hours despite being a better economic environment. There are 2 possible explanations:

1. Access to Modern Educational Infrastructure: Luxembourg government makes public education accessible to all by making it free. Singapore invests heavily in education to produce a skilled workforce and maintain their global competitiveness in the global economy. This creates a skilled workforce, improving productivity and income per capita.

2. European and Scandinavian Regional Welfare Culture: Many Scandinavian and European countries have developed extensive welfare states, providing social safety nets and emphasising on work-life balance.

Regional Differences

Would people work longer hours in better economic environments? It is dependent on the region and culture. European and Scandinavian regions work lesser hours despite being in a better economic environment. Singapore continues to experience long working hours despite being one of the best economic environments in SEA region.

Finding 02

Long Working Hours Poor Happiness

When it comes to first impressions of long working hours, people would associate with negative feelings or emotions. However, is that the case universally across countries or economies with long working hours? If not, how do countries with long working hours achieve such high positive emotions rating?

Observations

Figure 17: Top 5 countries with longest working hours and positive affect category

Figure 18: Life ladder overtime for top 5 longest working hours countries

Interestingly, Mexico, Costa Rica, Colombia and Singapore achieved Good and Excellent Positive Emotions category across despite long working hours. Only Turkey achieved Bad category for working long hours.

Happy to Work

3 out of the 4 countries with longest working hours achieving good and excellent positive emotions rating were from the Latin American continent while 1 is from South-East-Asia. This begs how do these countries achieve such positive emotions despite long working hours.

Singapore

Singapore achieved a respectable life ladder rating above 0.6, negative emotions rating less than 0.2 and positive emotions rating above 0.6. All these while having annual average working hours above 40. These results demonstrate Singaporeans are satisfied and contented.

A possible explanation is Singapore's safe and secure environment, stable government and high quality of life. Efficient law enforcement keeps Singapore streets safe against crime. With its particularly modern infrastructure and strong currency, it makes it a very lucrative environment to settle down for foreigners. Furthermore, Singapore has political stability with a political party elected since 1959 that has made wise decisions that made the country prosperous that continues till today.

Recent Policy Changes

While Singapore fails to achieve a higher life ladder rating like Finland, the government has made attempts to improve work-life balance. Starting December 1, 2024, all employers must consider workers' requests for flexible work arrangements under the new Tripartite Guidelines on FWAs. The 2024 National Day Rally saw the government increasing parental leave from 20 weeks to 30 weeks with employers mandated to four weeks of government-paid paternity leave (up from two).

Latin America: Colombia, Mexico & Costa Rica

For these 3 Latin American countries, the fact these countries achieve higher annual average working hours while achieving high levels of happiness can be attributed to 3 common factors:

1. Strong Social Connections and Family Values: This is part of Latin America culture. Family bonds provide emotional support that buffers against work stress.

2. Government Legislations: Presence of labour laws or codes to provide work-life balance.

3. Positive and Resilient Outlook: Latin America historically has struggled and overcame multiple economic, political crises and hardships like the 1980s debt crisis which widened society inequality. This shared experience has moulded resilience.

Longer working hours can be attributed to the need to provide economic necessity for families to avoid future hardships and unlock more opportunities to climb up the life ladder. One example from the World Economic Forum on Costa Rica is investing in education to produce human talent, which improves human well-being as providing better education unlocks better job opportunities and increases income.

Finding 03

Working Conditions Matter Towards Happiness

On the other hand, improved working conditions improve happiness. Improved working conditions can be an improvement to paid annual leaves, better severance pay in cases of dismissal, or both.

Paid Annual Leave: Time off from work where employees are entitled to receive pay from their employers during time off.

Severance Pay: Financial compensation that an employer provides to employees upon employee termination from work to help transit to either a new future job or unemployment. The compensation is a one-time offer.

Severance Pay Analysis

Figure 23: Histogram for severance pay at 1 year tenure

Figure 24: Histogram for severance pay at 5 tenure

Figure 25: Histogram for severance pay at 10 tenure

Majority Have No Severance Pay

From the histogram chart, most economies do not have severance pay for their employees. 23 out of 45 (51%) do not have severance pay at 1-year tenure. For 5- and 10-year tenure, the data is slightly better with reduction to 17 out of 45 (~38%).

Majority of countries not having severance pay for employees is largely attributed to a lack of government legislation mandating need for employers to provide severance pay in event of employee termination.

In Singapore's case, laws are more friendly towards employers than employees and workers who work continuously for less than 2 years with an employer are not entitled to any retrenchment benefit. According to Channel News Asia (CNA), unions like NTUC and SNEF have no legal force on employers to pressure them to issue severance package in event of employee termination.

How Do These Economies Fare on Life Ladder?

Figure 35: Life ladder overtime with paid annual leave for 1-year tenure (top and bottom 3)

Figure 36: Life ladder overtime with paid annual leave for 5-year tenure (top and bottom 3)

Does improvements to working conditions, in this case, paid annual leaves improve achieving the best possible life? A higher paid annual leave offers range of benefits that can improve quality of life personally and professionally — reduced stress, improved productivity and more exploration opportunities for personal growth.

For top 3 countries with highest paid annual leave for 1-year tenure, there is an upward trend improvement in Life Ladder, even though France life ladder lacks behind Finland and UK.

In comparison, Mexico and Singapore falls behind with poorer life ladder. This is attributed to their long working hours and lower paid annual leave. Despite suffering from no paid annual leave, US surprisingly does well for its life ladder, indicating other factors that help contribute to the final life ladder result.

At the 5 and 10-year tenure, Costa Rica replaces Mexico for one of the lowest paid annual leave. Singapore continues as one of the worst performing for its life ladder. Costa Rica saw better life ladder than Singapore even though they have very similar paid annual leave. US continues to show resilience with a better life ladder than Singapore and even France despite having no paid annual leave. Surprisingly, France lacks behind in life ladder, performing worse than the US and Costa Rica despite having much higher paid leave than them.

Insights

Paid Leave Helps

Improved working conditions like having paid annual leave can improve employees' chances of achieving best life possible for themselves like Finland. Singapore with its long working hours and limited employee protection lowers life ladder rating.

The US Exception

However, US with poor employee benefits can achieve higher life ladder than France, known for generous employee benefits and compensation. A possible explanation is the US work ethic, which places emphasis on hard work leading to greater accomplishments and unlocking future opportunities.

This means longer working hours than other countries but as workers in America hone their skills more, they gain better proficiency, increase their wages, generating higher income levels. This translates to unlocking more opportunities to pursue side hustles or hobbies and increase likelihood of achieving best life possible. This is particularly for businesses, specifically small businesses who enjoy tax breaks from the US government.

Furthermore, US work culture emphasises on entrepreneurship and innovation. Entrepreneurship is an individual who has an idea, acts on it, usually to disrupt the current market with a new product or service. An individual can take advantage of their personal interests, innovate and make money from it, generating income — which is another possible reason why life ladder is high even though employee benefits are poor.

Regarding employee benefits like paid leaves, there is no mandated legislation by the state or federal government on employers to pay for staff even when on leave.

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

Wealth Enables Happiness

Countries with higher GDP per capita consistently show higher life satisfaction, positive emotions, and lower negative emotions.

Hours Happiness

Long working hours don't automatically lead to unhappiness. Cultural context and economic opportunity matter more.

Conditions Count

Working conditions like paid leave improve life satisfaction, but cultural factors can compensate for their absence.

Relationship between work and happiness is not significant. Better working conditions does not necessarily justify to workers working longer as shown from results in Europe and Scandinavian regions. Countries with better GDP per capita have better life ladder ratings, higher positive emotions and lower negative emotions.

Furthermore, happiness is dependent on the region due to difference in region culture, making it a subjective topic to discuss since there is a different interpretation from every individual what is happiness. Thus, highlighting limitations of improved working conditions and emphasise complex nature of happiness and well-being.

People living in poorer economic environments are less likely to pursue their passions and more inclined to work longer to make ends meet. In comparison, people in wealthier societies have more opportunities to pursue hobbies or passions that make them happy.

Region is also a fundamental factor in determining working hours, culture and happiness. That is why Europe or Scandinavian regions can have lower working hours while Latin America and Asia regions work longer. Importantly, access to state-of-the-art facilities like prestigious universities produces skilled human capital, allowing higher productivity per hour per headcount in wealthier societies. Hence, lower working hours required.
