Introduction

The Global Stroke Crisis

Stroke is a critical illness characterised by poor blood flow to the brain, representing one of the most significant public health challenges worldwide.

Individuals who contract stroke either face lethal death or long-term disability if they survive. Either way, stroke brings significant economic and social burden to individuals, families and loved ones. The advancement in medical technology in the healthcare industry has allowed for earlier detection of stroke, enabling creation and implementation of preventive measures.

Problem Statement
How can we accurately predict the likelihood of a patient experiencing stroke based on their health records?

Using a data-driven machine learning solution, this project seeks to address the need for robust and interpretable predictive models on complex individual patient risk profiles, assisting healthcare providers in proactive stroke risk assessment.

Data Preparation

To address the 201 missing BMI values, median imputation was performed, replacing null values with median values to preserve data integrity while being robust to outliers.

Class Imbalance Challenge
The dataset shows signs of highly significant class imbalance with only 4.87% being stroke cases. SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) was considered but ultimately not used due to concerns about the model learning from artificial patterns rather than genuine clinical presentations, and potential overfitting risks. This is accepted as a limitation of the project.

As people age, they become more susceptible to chronic and critical illness. Higher average glucose levels increase stroke likelihood as people get older. Interestingly, patients suffer stroke at lower BMI but at much higher age — possibly because older individuals lose weight due to decreased appetite while their blood vessels narrow, reducing flow to vital organs like the brain.

Lifestyle Factors

Figure 7: Stroke Rate by Employment Status

Figure 8: Stroke Rate by Smoking Status & Age

People who are unemployed never suffered a stroke in this dataset, while those employed face higher likelihood. For smoking status, those who currently smoke or formerly smoked face higher stroke rates, compounded with age. In the 60-69 age group, stroke likelihood reaches 11.3% for former smokers and 9.1% for current smokers.

Figure 9: Stroke Rate by Age Group and Residence Type

Regardless of residence type, stroke rate increases with age. People in urban residences tend to have slightly higher stroke rates in certain age groups (50-59, 70+), possibly due to faster-paced lifestyle and higher stress. However, rural residents show comparable rates, highlighting the complexity of factors influencing stroke.

Key Risk Factors Identified
Age, elevated glucose levels, hypertension, heart disease, and smoking history show strong correlations with stroke likelihood — aligning closely with established medical knowledge.

Best Choice

Critical Finding: The Accuracy Trap
Despite Gradient Boosting's impressive 95.1% accuracy, it achieves only 8% recall — missing 92% of actual stroke patients. The model learned to predict "no stroke" for almost everyone, inflating accuracy but making it clinically useless for identifying at-risk patients. In medical diagnosis, a false negative (missing a stroke case) is far more dangerous than a false positive.

Logistic Regression predicted 244 false positives (healthy patients flagged as at-risk) compared to Gradient Boosting's 4. However, it also produced only 10 false negatives versus Gradient Boosting's catastrophic 46 false negatives. In healthcare, it is better to be safe than sorry.

For stroke prediction, catching at-risk patients is paramount. An 80% recall means the model provides the best safety net for identifying patients who need medical attention, even if it means some healthy patients receive false alarms.

Deployment

Recommended Framework

The developed Logistic Regression model can serve as a first-layer screening tool in various healthcare settings, providing a three-tiered risk categorization system.

Risk Categorization

Low Risk

< 30%

Standard preventive care recommendations. Routine monitoring and healthy lifestyle guidance.

Medium Risk

30 - 70%

Enhanced monitoring and lifestyle intervention programs. More frequent check-ups recommended.

High Risk

> 70%

Priority referral to stroke prevention specialists. Immediate medical intervention required.

Example Case
A 58-year-old hypertensive male with BMI 25.3 and glucose 92.79 mg/dL is classified as Medium Risk (64.42% probability). This empowers the patient to take charge of their health and visit healthcare centres accordingly, where licensed professionals maintain full authority to override model recommendations based on clinical judgment.

Deployment Settings

Primary Care - Routine check-ups & annual exams
Emergency Departments - Symptom triage

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

This project successfully developed a machine learning-based stroke prediction system prioritizing clinical utility over conventional performance metrics.

80% Recall

Logistic Regression catches 4 out of 5 stroke patients, providing the best safety net for medical screening.

0.83 ROC-AUC

Strong ability to distinguish between stroke and non-stroke cases across all threshold settings.

Clinical Alignment

Risk factors align with established medical knowledge: age, glucose, hypertension, heart disease, smoking.

In medicine, it is indeed better to be safe than sorry. Catching at-risk patients, even with false alarms, is far preferable to missing actual stroke cases.

Limitations

1

Limited Dataset: Only 5,110 patients from a single source, limiting generalisability across diverse populations.

2

Class Imbalance: Severe imbalance with only 4.87% stroke cases constrains model learning despite balanced class weighting.

3

Limited Features: Excludes potentially important predictors such as family history, current medications, and detailed lifestyle factors.

Future Work

Priority research directions include assembling larger, more diverse datasets through multi-institutional collaboration at national levels, and expanding features to include family history, medications, and detailed lifestyle factors for deeper insights.
