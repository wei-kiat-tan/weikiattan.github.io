The Data

Understanding the Dataset

Yummy is an online restaurant booking platform where users can sign up for free to make reservations via its app. Yummy earns revenue from restaurant commissions on bookings and advertisements displayed on the app. The dataset contains 1,000 rows with 4 key variables.

The variables are: expn (average dining expenses per diner per reservation in 1 month prior to launching the paid membership program), rsvn (reservations made in 1 month prior to launch), disc (discount amount: 0 = no discount, 1 = 10%, 2 = 20%), and convert (whether users converted to paid membership: 0 = never, 1 = within 2 weeks, 2 = after 2 weeks).

Key Trends

What the Data Reveals

Figure 3: Percentage breakdown of conversions by discount level

Trend 01

Higher Discounts = Higher Conversions

The data shows a clear pattern: 12.81% converted with no discount (out of 320 people), 27.58% with 10% discount, and 35.71% with 20% discount.

The jump from 0% to 10% discount increased conversions by 14.77%. Going from no discount to 20% increased by 22.91%. The difference between 10% and 20% discount groups is 8.13%.

Key Takeaway

Discounts entice existing users to convert to permanent membership. This is supported by the correlation matrix value of 0.21 between membership conversion and discounts.

Trend 02

Target Low-to-Medium Reservation Users

Based on the data, there is a higher number of customers in the lower range of reservations. Most users make 1-8 reservations per month.

Low (1-3): First-time or infrequent users â€” offer attractive limited-time incentives to get them to reserve more often.

Moderate (4-6): Some experience but not frequent â€” focus on retention incentives to keep them using the app.

High (7+): Regular users â€” consider loyalty programmes and referral incentives to friends or family members who haven't used Yummy.

This strategy not only allows Yummy to retain regular users but expand their app user numbers through referrals.

Trend 03

Discounts Don't Increase Reservations

Surprisingly, discounts don't make users book more often. The median reservation count is 7 across all discount groups.

Discount group 2 (20%) and no discount group both have the smallest Interquartile Range (IQR) of 8, indicating lower variability in reservation numbers than the 10% discount group. All groups have minimum reservations of 1.

The IQR ranges across all discount groups are very similar, reinforcing the idea that customers feel indifferent about making more reservations even with discounts offered.

Important

Discounts are not an influential factor on customer behaviour in encouraging them to make more reservations on Yummy. When customers don't use the app often, they are less inclined to take up paid membership. Discounts influence conversion decisions, not booking behavior.

Part B

Business Objective & Marketing Strategy

Based on Yummy's tiered membership discount promotion targeting existing app users, the business objective is to convert existing users to paid membership users and increase platform usage for more reservations through a timed sales tiered discount scheme.

The tiered membership discount model aims to convert existing users by providing better customer experience through enhanced dining experiences. This promotes customer loyalty and enhances engagement through premium features for paid users. Since Yummy earns revenue from restaurant commissions, higher spending coupled with higher reservations will increase revenues.

A 2-week timeline is optimal for higher conversions as shown in Figure 10. The discounts were targeted to users who have made spending at restaurants to convert them to paid users.

Marketing Strategy Effects

The effects can be identified through: (1) higher conversion rates when higher discounts are offered, (2) existing users with higher reservations having higher probability to convert when given discounts, (3) average dining expenses between $100-$300 showing highest conversions. However, there could be other factors affecting users' decisions to convert not reflected in the data.

Part D: Experiment Design

RCT with Stratification

To maximize conversions to paid membership by offering discounts, we designed a Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) with Stratification. This allows analysis of the relationship between conversion rates and discount rates while controlling other variables.

We test with 999 users (chosen for even 3-way splitting) across three discount rates: 0% (control group), 10%, and 20%. The total discount budget is equivalent to 103 membership fees, with maximum 20% discount per user offered at most once.

Stratification Process

To ensure balanced representation, we group users based on two key variables: average dining expenses (expn) and number of reservations (rsvn).

Experiment Considerations

Limitation: Out of Bounds

Linear regression can give results outside 0-1 range (e.g., "1.2" or "-0.5"). A conversion of 1.2 implies 120% probability, which is impossible. These out-of-bounds results may complicate interpretation and limit model reliability.
